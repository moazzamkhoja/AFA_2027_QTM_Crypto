## Decision 1: Light Formalization, Not a Full Structural Model

Two options were considered for Section 2:

- **Option A — Full structural model.** Build a new agent-optimization/equilibrium model
  from scratch (e.g., search-theoretic, multi-period) deriving our predictions formally,
  with full propositions and proofs. High rigor, but multi-week effort and high risk of
  redundancy with existing models (Kiyotaki-Wright, Sockin-Xiong).
- **Option B — Applied theory with light formalization.** Motivate the empirical signal
  using existing theory, with brief stylized one-period models that yield clean,
  citable propositions mapping directly onto the hypotheses. Standard approach in
  empirical asset-pricing papers built around a new signal (e.g., Liu-Tsyvinski-Wu).

**Decision: Option B.** Rationale: the paper's contribution is the empirical decomposition
and signal, not a new structural model; the AFA special session rewards a documented,
efficient AI-assisted research process over a maximally rigorous theory section; the H2
interaction (signal strongest when unpriced) is a price-discovery story that is easier to
motivate informally than to derive from a fully specified equilibrium model.

## Topic: The $F(\cdot)$ Aggregation Critique and Governance-Token Staking Mechanics

## Decision 3: Reject the Closed-Form Aggregation, Then Properly Fix It

Human read the drafted Section 2.1 and rejected the original aggregation step
$\lambda_t = F(s_t + \bar c_{t+1})$: substituting the cross-sectional *average* convenience
yield $\bar c_{t+1}$ into the CDF of $\varphi_i$ inside $F$ does not follow from the
individual threshold condition $s_t + c_{i,t+1} \geq \varphi_i$ unless $c_{i,t+1}$ is common
to all agents — which would eliminate the private-information premise the whole signal
relies on. Human asked for the underlying "trick" to be explained.

Explained the rigorous fix: a continuum of agents with a common unobserved future state
$\theta_{t+1}$, private noisy signals $x_i = \theta_{t+1}+\varepsilon_i$, and idiosyncratic
liquidity costs $\varphi_i$, with a law-of-large-numbers argument washing out idiosyncratic
noise in the aggregate. This was first proposed as background only ("why your intuition is
sound") with a recommendation *not* to add it to the paper, since it conflicts with the
Option B (light formalization) scope. Human's interim instruction was to keep it simple and
flag the result as an assumption to be tested empirically — implemented as
"Proposition 1 (Assumption)."

On reflection, human asked for two further fixes in the same pass:

1. **Small fix** — generalize Section 2.2's governance-token cost structure. The model's
   $\bar q = (\ell+\kappa)/\Delta$ already nested the no-lock case ($\ell=0$), but the prose
   didn't say so. Added a paragraph making explicit that vote-escrow protocols (Curve veCRV,
   Balancer veBAL) have $\ell>0$ (a real illiquidity cost), while snapshot/balance-voting
   protocols with no lock (Uniswap, Compound) have $\ell=0$, leaving $\kappa$ (monitoring/
   engagement cost) as the only force sustaining the separating equilibrium. Same threshold
   logic, different observable action.
2. **Big fix** — replaced the unjustified $F(s_t+\bar c_{t+1})$ *and* the bare
   "Proposition 1 (Assumption)" hand-wave with the actual continuum-of-agents derivation:
   $c_{i,t+1} = \theta_{t+1}+\varepsilon_i$ (common state + private noise), threshold
   $\eta_i \equiv \varphi_i-\varepsilon_i \leq s_t+\theta_{t+1}$, and SLLN aggregation giving
   $\lambda_t = H(s_t+\theta_{t+1})$ for a fixed increasing $H$ — a genuinely *derived*
   result, not an assumption. The one remaining assumption is narrowly scoped: that
   recovering $\theta_{t+1}$ from $\lambda_t$ requires costly information extraction
   (Grossman-Stiglitz 1980), so price only partially reflects it at $t$. Proposition 1 is
   now stated as a clean proposition again (label "(Assumption)" removed), with the
   partial-revelation step called out explicitly as the model's one substantive assumption.

## Decision 4: $\lambda_t$ as a Measurement Index, Not a Single Formula

Human's question — "why would someone stake a governance token, what does staking even
mean, and should holding period be a $\lambda$ measure if people don't vote?" — was
researched via web search. Findings: governance "staking" spans three distinct mechanisms.
(1) Vote-escrow locking (Curve veCRV, Balancer veBAL, Aerodrome ve(3,3)): tokens locked up to
several years for voting power plus a real fee/yield share — genuine illiquidity cost.
(2) Yield-bearing staking with no lock requirement (GMX): stakers earn a direct share of
protocol fees; governance rights often attach to holding rather than staking — closer to the
coin model's seigniorage channel than to costly signaling. (3) Pure snapshot/balance voting,
no staking at all (Uniswap, Compound, Aave's base layer): voting power equals token balance,
fully liquid, gasless off-chain voting. Category (3) means the existing $\lambda$ definition
(governance staked / circulating) is literally undefined for several planned-universe tokens
(UNI, COMP, AAVE).

Human's resolution: $\lambda_t$-as-conviction is the right concept, but the *empirical
measurement* should be an index over three observable channels — staking/locking, holding
period, and voting engagement — with the specific construction (weights, thresholds, which
channels apply to which protocol) decided later, once we are looking at the actual data,
rather than fixed now in the theory section. This is now stated directly in Section 2.2 (see
Decision 3, small fix) as a forward pointer to Section~\ref{sec:data}, rather than as a fixed
formula in the theoretical framework. No empirical design choice was finalized in this
session; only the principle that $\lambda$ is multi-channel was confirmed.
